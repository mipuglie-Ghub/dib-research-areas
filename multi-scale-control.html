Multi-Scale Control of Large Multi-Agent Systems

Many modern systems — robot swarms, biological populations, traffic flows, and social systems — cannot be effectively controlled without delving into the interplay between agents dynamics and collective behaviors. Our group develops multi-scale control architectures that explicitly bridge microscopic agent dynamics and macroscopic population-level behavior. A central concept in this research is continuification: the derivation and use of continuum models (e.g., density and flux equations) to design controllers that scale with population size while preserving control-theoretic guarantees. Control actions designed at the macroscopic level are systematically translated into microscopic actuation strategies through principled macro–micro and micro–macro bridges, including optimal transport, spatial sampling, and density estimation.

Shepherding, a classical problem from swarm robotics, and leader–follower systems serve as canonical testbeds for this framework. In these systems, a small number of controlled agents (leaders or herders) shape the collective behavior of a much larger population through indirect, often non-reciprocal interactions. Our work shows how such sparse actuation mechanisms can be exploited to control arbitrary density patterns, distributions, and collective motions.

Core research topics

  • Continuification and multi-scale modeling
  • Macro–micro and micro–macro control architectures
  • Shepherding control at the micro and macro level
  • Nonreciprocal interactions and field theory of decision-making
  • Mean-field and kinetic approaches to large populations
  • Optimal transport for coverage control and macro-micro bridges
  • Sparse control via reinforcement learning

Related applications

Multi-robot coordination, swarm robotics, human coordination, collective animal behavior, traffic flow control
